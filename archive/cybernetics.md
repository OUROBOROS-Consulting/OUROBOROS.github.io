    why certain patterns repeat across wildly different contexts, and why institutions so often
    protect themselves at the expense of the people they're supposed to serve.

sections:
  - id: cybernetics
    heading: Cybernetics
    body_paragraphs:
      - >
        The word comes from the Greek kybernetes — helmsman, the one who steers.
        Norbert Wiener coined the modern term in 1948 to describe the study of purposive, self-regulating systems.
        The central insight: systems maintain themselves through feedback. They sense their state,
        compare it to a target, and correct. A thermostat. A living cell. An immune response.
      - >
        What makes cybernetics powerful — and unsettling — is that it applies just as well to social systems.
        Organizations, relationships, and institutions are all feedback systems. They have set points they
        defend, signals they amplify, and deviations they suppress. Understanding which is which
        tells you a great deal about whose interests a system actually serves.
      - >
        Gregory Bateson extended Wiener's ideas into anthropology and psychology, arguing that mind itself
        is a cybernetic process — that thought, perception, and behavior are all forms of information
        processing in a loop with the environment. Pathology, on this view, is not a property of
        a person but of a system: a pattern that persists because it's being maintained.
      - >
        This framing changed how I understand everything I've been through. The abuse wasn't random.
        The institutional failures weren't coincidental. They were systems behaving exactly as designed —
        optimizing for their own continuity rather than for the people inside them.

  - id: feedback
    heading: Feedback Loops
    body_paragraphs:
      - >
        A negative feedback loop is stabilizing: deviation from the target triggers a correction.
        Body temperature, blood sugar, the steering of a ship — these are all negative feedback systems.
        They are the architecture of homeostasis, the mechanism by which living things stay alive.
      - >
        A positive feedback loop is amplifying: deviation from the target triggers more deviation.
        This is not inherently bad — positive feedback drives growth, learning, and change.
        But unchecked, it produces runaway dynamics. Compound interest. Viral spread. Escalating conflict.
        The rich getting richer. A personality disorder getting worse with every enabling response.
      - >
        Most of the systems that have failed me operate through positive feedback loops dressed up
        as neutral procedures. An institution that rewards silence produces more silence.
        A relationship where manipulation is never named produces a partner who manipulates more.
        A legal system that equates credibility with composure produces survivors who learn to perform
        composure — and abusers who perform it better.

  - id: examples
    heading: Examples of Positive Feedback Loops
    body_paragraphs:
      - >
        Intergenerational trauma is a positive feedback loop. Unprocessed harm in one generation
        creates the conditions — neurological, behavioral, relational — that make harm more likely
        in the next. It persists not because people choose it, but because the system has no
        corrective mechanism. Therapy, awareness, and documentation are attempts to introduce one.
      - >
        Institutional inertia is a positive feedback loop. Policies that protect institutions from
        accountability make those institutions harder to reform, which entrenches the policies further.
        The people harmed by those institutions are routinely told to use internal processes —
        processes that were designed by, and for, the institution itself.
      - >
        The Ouroboros — the snake consuming its own tail — is the oldest symbol of this dynamic.
        It represents cycles that are self-sustaining by design: the abuser who cycles through
        idealization and devaluation, the PhD program that delays a student's progress while
        claiming to support them, the legal system that grants a protective order while failing
        to enforce it. The loop continues until someone names it, documents it, and refuses
        to participate in it anymore.
